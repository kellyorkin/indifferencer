About the Game

The Game That Has a Verdict.
Unlike the Meetings.

The Corporate Courtroom: The Debriefings is a standalone adversarial card game set in the most dangerous arena known to professional life: a corporate legal proceeding in which everyone has an agenda, no one has all the facts, and the client had a simple question two years ago that somehow ended up here.

A companion to Meetings: The Blathering, The Debriefings shares its universe of resigned corporate absurdism but introduces a tighter, more gameable structure. Where Meetings sprawls — because meetings sprawl — a courtroom has a beginning, a middle, and an end.

Someone wins. Someone loses. The chaos resolves. This is the cathartic counterpoint. The client still does not know the answer to their original question.

Core Mechanics

How the Case Fails You

⚖
Camp System
Prosecution and Defense function as opposing camps — each with internal hierarchy, aligned incentives, and the ability to play cards across the aisle. The adversarial relationship is structural. You are explicitly attempting to dismantle the other side's case while your own holds up under cross-examination. Neutral cards serve the court, not a camp.
◈
Verdict Tokens
At the end of each round, the camp with the higher total ATK wins the round and places one Verdict Token in the jury box. First camp to accumulate 3 Verdict Tokens wins. The Jury Foreperson holds the token physically — control of the Foreperson is a legitimate strategic target. The client is not a strategic target. The client is simply there.
↺
Cross-Examination
The stakeholder meeting equivalent. Force your opponent to reveal their full hand. You choose one card to discard. Can be initiated once per round by the Prosecutor. The person who walked in confident leaves visibly diminished. This is the intended outcome.
⟁
The Product Lifecycle Trilogy
Subpoena → Contempt → Evidence → Appeals. Played in sequence, each card buffs the next. Completing the chain triggers The Full Lifecycle: opponent discards 2 cards, the round extends one additional turn. Nobody remembers what product it was for. The client still does not know the answer to their original question.
